Ford GoBike System Data — Explanatory Analysis

Dataset: February 2019  |  Trips analyzed: 182,803  |  Source: Ford GoBike System Data

Investigation Overview

Central Question: How do different types of Ford GoBike users in the San Francisco Bay Area use the system, and what patterns emerge from their trip behavior?

This analysis presents the three most compelling findings from the exploratory phase, each supported by a polished visualization.

Dataset Overview

The Ford GoBike dataset covers all individual bike-share trips in the greater San Francisco Bay Area during February 2019. The original dataset contained 183,412 trips and 16 features. After removing extreme duration outliers (>3 hours) and implausible rider ages, 182,803 trips were retained.

Finding 1: Subscribers Take Short Commute Trips — Customers Take Leisurely Rides

Figure 1: Trip duration density comparison — Subscribers vs. Customers (log scale). Median lines annotated.

Key Insight: Subscribers have a sharp, tall distribution peaking around 8 minutes — consistent with short commute hops between transit stations. Customers have a flatter, wider distribution peaking around 13 minutes, consistent with leisurely city exploration. The median for Customers (13.2 min) is 61% higher than for Subscribers (8.2 min). This difference is consistent regardless of gender or day of week.

Finding 2: Subscribers Are Commuters on Weekdays, Tourists on Weekends

Figure 2: Average hourly trips by user type, split by Weekday vs. Weekend.

Key Insight: On weekdays, Subscribers show twin rush-hour spikes at 8 AM and 5 PM — a textbook commuter signature. On weekends, Subscriber behavior shifts dramatically to a single midday peak, resembling the Customer pattern. Customers maintain a consistent midday pattern 7 days a week. This confirms the system serves two roles: daily transit infrastructure for subscribers and recreational/tourist exploration for customers.

Finding 3: User Type Dominates Gender in Predicting Trip Duration

Figure 3: Median trip duration by gender and user type. Error bars show IQR (Q1–Q3).

Key Insight: Across all three genders, Customer median durations are consistently ~5 minutes longer than Subscriber medians. Within each user type, gender differences are modest (<2 min). Female customers take the longest median trips (~14.0 min); male subscribers the shortest (~8.1 min). The error bars (IQR) confirm Customers have far more variability. User type is the primary predictor of trip duration; gender is secondary.

Conclusion: The Ford GoBike system in February 2019 was primarily a commuter utility. The subscriber–customer divide is the single most important dimension in understanding usage patterns, explaining differences in trip duration, timing, and day-of-week behavior more powerfully than demographic factors like age or gender.
